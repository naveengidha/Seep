♠ Seep

2-player card game  ·  Quick-start guide

Goal

Score the most points by capturing valuable cards and earning bonus points for clearing the table (seeps).

Setup

  • Use a standard 52-card deck.
  • Deal 4 cards to each player.
  • Place 4 cards face-up in the center — this is the floor.
  • Put the remaining deck face-down.
  • After both players use their 4 cards, deal another 4 each.
  • Continue until the deck is exhausted.

Keep captured cards face-down in your own scoring pile.

All captures are based on matching totals.

Examples:
· 7 captures a single 7
· 9 captures 4 + 5
· King (13) captures 6 + 7

You may capture multiple groups with one card if each group matches the played card.

Multi-capture example:
Floor: 3, 5, 8  →  Play an 8 to capture both (3 + 5) and (8)

Your Turn — Choose ONE Action

1. Capture

Play a card from your hand and take matching cards from the floor.

Example: Floor: 2, 4, 6  →  Play a 10 to capture 4 + 6

2. Build a House

Combine a card from your hand with cards on the floor to create a set value that you plan to capture later.

Example: Floor: 4  ·  Hand: 6 and 10
Play the 6 with the 4 to create a house worth 10.
You must already hold the 10 to capture it later.
⚠ Your opponent can steal your house if they can capture it first.

For your first few games, skip houses until everyone understands capturing.

3. Discard

If you cannot or do not want to capture or build, place one card face-up on the floor.

What Is a Seep?

A seep happens when you capture every card currently on the floor, leaving it empty.

Example: Floor: 2, 5, 6
Play a King (13) and capture all three cards (2 + 5 + 6 = 13).
The floor is now empty — you scored a seep bonus.

Agree before starting how many bonus points a seep is worth. Many groups use +50.

Beginner Strategy Tips

Click a tip to expand it.

Every card you play must either total to a floor card or sum with floor cards to make the played card's value. Before your turn, scan the floor for all pairs and groups that reach your hand cards' values. The King (13) is especially powerful — look for floor groups that sum to 13.
When you discard, think about what you're leaving behind. A lone 7 or a pair that easily sums to a common hand card is a gift to your opponent. Prefer discarding cards that create awkward totals or that your opponent is unlikely to hold.
Spades score points — especially J♠ (11), Q♠ (12), and K♠ (13). If a valuable spade lands on the floor, prioritize capturing it over other plays. If it's in your hand, be strategic about when you play it.
A house requires you to hold the capture card in your hand. If your opponent can reach the same total, they steal the whole house — including your contributed card. Only build when you can capture on your very next turn, or when the floor state makes a steal very unlikely.
A seep is worth +50 points — potentially as much as half the cards in the deck. If the floor is near-empty (1–2 cards), think about whether your opponent can clear it on their turn. Discard a card that makes the floor total unreachable if you must pass.
Most mistakes happen because players focus only on the current turn. Ask yourself: what will the floor look like after I play? What cards might my opponent hold? Can I set up a seep or a multi-capture next turn? For practice, play open-handed and discuss each move out loud.
